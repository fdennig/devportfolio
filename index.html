about

I am assistant professor at Yale-NUS College in Singapore. I am also associate researcher with the Climate Futures Initiative at the Princeton Environmental Institute. During the 2018/19 academic year I am visiting Stanford University as NUS fellow at the Center for Advanced Study in the Behavioral Sciences.

I am interested primarily in economic policy questions relating to climate change and inequality. For example, it is possible that climate change as well as climate policy will affect poor people disproportionately. Climate policy might thereby have an important impact on inequality. With one group of co-authors I have looked at how this is taken into account in the evaluation of climate policy. I am also interested in understanding other determinants of economic inequality. With another group of colleagues I am investigating other long term determinants of inequality. How does family planning affect the distribution of capital in the long run; how does the market structure in the supply of education affect the distribution and the returns to human capital. My entire research agenda is based on the motivation to develop policy solutions to address these important issues.

teaching

Introduction to game theory

This is a first year course intended to illustrate important concepts from game theory such as Nash equilibrium, backward induction, and sequential rationality to students with little or no knowledge of economics.

Public economics

I cover standard topics in public finance and economic policy making with some emphasis on normative foundations. I require some economics or university level calculus.

Quantitative reasoning

In this compulsory first year course we teach students "how to convince themselves and others of an assertion". We cover basic frequentist statistics, mathematical logic, and linear regression.

Intermediate microeconomics

This is a standard course in microeconomics covering consumer theory (with some behavioural concepts), producer theory, and competitive markets.
